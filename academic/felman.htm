Alex Bleasdale                                          

Fantasy, Culture and Society                                             

4/11/99

 

What can we understand by the following statements made by Shoshana Felman: -

 

‘In the same way that psychoanalysis points to the unconscious of literature, literature, in its turn, is the unconscious of psychoanalysis’

(‘To open the question’: 10)

 

‘The question underlying such a reading [of Turn of the Screw] is thus not “what does the story mean?” but rather “how does the story mean?”’ (‘Turning the Screw of Interpretation’: 119)

 

  My decision to examine both of Felman’s statements has imposed – and to some degree, created – a structure for my essay.  I intend to use the first statement as a starting point and to gravitate towards the second statement in due course with a critical analysis of some of the shared terms that serve to forge links between both statements.  It is my belief that a degree of overlap exists between them, and therefore my intention is to investigate both statements with the intention of identifying some of these linking factors as my essay plays out.

 

  Felman opens the question with the initial claim that ‘while literature is considered as a body of language – to be interpreted – psychoanalysis is considered as a body of knowledge, whose competence is called upon to interpret’ (1982:5).  She broadly categorises psychoanalysis as subject and literature as object.  This is set further in place by applying a master/slave structure to them; there is a dynamic encounter, which becomes a ‘fight for recognition’ where the authority of psychoanalysis is called upon – as a subject - to play the analyst.  The text becomes the analysand, and serves the subject’s desire for recognition.  Psychoanalysis therefore exercises both authority and power over the text, satisfying itself by ‘holding a discourse of masterly competence’ (ibid:6).  Therefore psychoanalysis points to the unconscious of literature.

 

  The initial master/slave ‘frame of relationship’ - in Felman’s terms – quickly becomes problematic when any equal (or ‘real’) dialogue occurs between psychoanalysis and literature (literature, in literary terms, is a body of knowledge and psychoanalysis is a body of language).  Therefore the ‘monologic’ structure can be - and for Felman, is - reversed from a literary perspective where the literary text can take the authoritative role.  Finally, through applying a linguistic structure to psychoanalysis, she suggests that the binary structure of [master/slave] opposition should be deconstructed; that literary analysis can be conducted irrespective of choosing between the roles of analyst and analysand.

 

  So Felman identifies an inherent paradox: on the one hand, the process of literary analysis resembles that of psychoanalysis.  On the other, the status of the literary text can resemble the master: the ‘subject presumed to know’.  Also, the process of reading, it is argued, can function using the same parameters of authority as described by Lacan in his definition of the ‘role of the psychoanalyst in the structure of transference’ (ibid:7).  On reading, the literary critic occupies two roles: the analyst in interpretation and the analysand in relation to transference from the text.  I will later examine the functioning of transference in relation to Felman’s second statement.

 

  Irony, Felman suggests, is a literary device that further deconstructs the ‘temptation’ to map a master/slave opposition onto an interaction between literature and psychoanalysis.  The nature of irony, Felman argues, ‘consists in dragging authority as such into a scene which it cannot master’ (ibid:8), that the ‘ironic force’ behind a literary text must, by virtue of its ironic nature, deconstruct any existing ‘fantasy of authority’ for the same reasons.  Therefore, application of the master/slave model to an ironic literary moment renders false any ‘professional’ (or masterful) claims made by psychoanalysis.  I will develop this point again later in this essay.

 

  In To Open the Question, Felman is urging a shift of emphasis, one that rules out ‘application’ of psychoanalysis over literature and gestures towards ‘implication’: involving psychoanalysis with literary analysis.  By doing so, ‘the interpreter’s role would here be (…) to act as a go-between, to generate implications between literature and psychoanalysis ’ (ibid:9).  In this way, Felman observes that although both ‘domains’ can be seen to inform each other, each are ‘also affected, displaced, by the other’ (ibid.).  Felman then states that literature is the ‘inherent reference by which psychoanalysis names its findings’ and argues that – despite their differences – they are ‘enfolded within’ each other; that ‘there are no natural boundaries between (…) which clearly define and distinguish them’ (ibid.).  If this is taken to be true (and Felman certainly argues its case), then it is possible to see how each domain can threaten the interiority of its other.  The enfolding and overlapping between the two domains means that ‘each is contained in the other as its otherness-to-itself’, or its unconscious[1].  Here Felman now makes her statement, that by nature of the two domains enfolding within each other, each informing and displacing each other, because there are no natural boundaries, if ‘psychoanalysis points to the unconscious of literature’, and the ‘conscious’ in literature forms the basis of psychoanalytical case studies, literature therefore takes place as the ‘unconscious’ basis functioning behind psychoanalytic theory.

 

  In To Open the Question, Felman articulates her own standpoint by adopting Lacanian visions of psychoanalysis (and of the analyst)[2]: ‘for Lacan, indeed, the unconscious is not only that which must be read, but also, and primarily, that which reads’ (p.118).  Therefore the reader adopts the role of the analyst on interpreting (and understanding) the text consciously, but also adopts the role of the analysand in the process of transference from the text to the unconscious.  James’s novel is primarily a useful text to consider in light of this model of reading precisely because of what is left open and understated for the unconscious.  The open nature of the text means it represents a power struggle for the reader; it was designed, in James’s own words, as ‘an amusette to catch those not easily caught’ (cited in Felman, 1982:102).  Subsequent critical readings into the ambiguities of the text have resulted in unwitting participation in the text; the trap set by James has resulted in re-enactments of the main conflicts indicated by the text.  In other words: the scene of the critical debates surrounding the text, have resembled repetitions of the conflicts within the text. 

 

  So what happens in Turn of the Screw?  Ambiguities in the text (and we can also consider these later in terms of authorial and analytical ‘authority’) are reflected in our response to it.  Here Felman asks the potent question: ‘are reading and ambiguity in any way compatible?’ (p.117).  With this statement in mind: is a reading of Turn of the Screw therefore possible without a performance of the same debates and conflicts thrown up by the text?  Lapanche and Pontalis describe the compulsion to repeat as ‘an ungovernable process originating in the unconscious’ (1973:78).  As Freud explains: ‘a thing which has not been understood inevitably reappears; like an unlaid ghost, it cannot rest until the mystery has been solved and the spell broken’ (ibid.79).  So when Felman observes that the scene of the critical debate is a repetition of the debate at stake in the text (i.e. it mirrors that in James’s story), the apparent ‘trap’ becomes clearly visible; any debate surrounding Turn of the Screw is therefore bound to replicate the debates and conflicts in the novel.  Textual ambiguities doom its critics as the process of transference takes place from text to subconscious.  Through a reading of the text, the text acts itself out.  Felman explains: ‘Indeed it is not so much the critic who comprehends the text, as the text which comprehends the critic’ (1982:115). 

 

  Authority is an issue of concern to Felman.  Critics call upon the work of Freud as an authority for psychoanalysis (although this I will later argue has changed in the light of Lacanian analysis), and James’s authority exists as the author of the novel and consequently the authority responsible for the opening of the debates surrounding his text.  Is our approach to both authors somehow different?  Does James’s text allow us to answer for James?  Does Freud’s text allow us to answer for him?  When considering questions concerning authority, these can only be addressed in terms of ‘the act of reading and of interpretation’ (ibid:116).  Freud’s authority, Felman argues, unlike James’s, is given weight by both Freudian and anti-Freudians; by configuring themselves in polar opposition over James’s ‘true meaning’ they are demonstrating agreement over the [ambiguous] ‘true meaning’ of Freud.  As a result, both viewpoints can be seen as ‘Freudian’ precisely because they are attacking Freud.

 

  There is a linguistic irony functioning here: that Freud’s ghost haunts both opposing critical viewpoints.  Reference to Freud can only exist in terms of reference, by this I mean they use Freud’s name to back their arguments.  They do not, however, use Freud’s psychoanalytical ‘authority’ because, as Felman points out, ‘the “true Freud” is no more immediately accessible to us than the “true James”’.  Freud’s ‘texts’, like James’s novel, convey ‘not an established truth or a referential knowledge, but an invitation to interpretation’ (ibid.).  Any Freudian reading can only be a reading of Freud’s knowledge, an interpretation, and as Freud lays himself open to criticism, by virtue of the speculative nature of his work, so any reading of Freud must equally find itself ‘threatened by error’ (ibid.).  Therefore ‘Freudian’ reading is always at risk of distorting Freud’s work because the circumstances in which Freud wrote cannot be the same as the circumstances in which it is used to map onto an [wild] analysis of any other body of literature.  So any Freudian reading can be justified as a truth, ‘but no truer than the opposed positions which contradict it.  And it is “false,” indeed, to the extent that it excludes them’ (ibid.117).

 

  To illustrate another of issues raised around what Felman criticises as Freudian ‘wild’ analysis of Turn of the Screw, I’d like to draw upon a letter written to Freud by Jung.  After several years of ongoing correspondence (and friendship) between Freud and Jung, Jung is finally moved to telling Freud what he really thinks of his practices.  I have no exact date for the letter, but my guess is it’s circa late 1912:

 

I am objective enough to see through your little trick. You go about sniffing out all the symptomatic actions in your vicinity, thus reducing everyone to the level of sons and daughters who blushingly admit the existence of their faults. Meanwhile you remain on top as the father, sitting pretty. For sheer obsequiousness nobody dares to pluck the prophet by the beard and inquire for once what you would say to a patient with a tendency to analyze the analyst instead of himself. You would certainly ask him: "who's got the neurosis?" (McGuire, 1974:534-535).

 

  This quote can be considered important because like Lacan, Jung notices fundamental flaws in Freud’s way of working.  In relation to Turn of the Screw, this seems to be largely to do with the effects of transference.  Lacan was largely interested in the process of transference: the object relation in the act of psychoanalysis between the analyst and analysand.  Freud, it seems has failed to recognise the depth at which transference operates.  This, it could be argued, is perhaps the major stumbling block for Wilson’s violent Freudian analysis of Turn of the Screw.  The argument Jung poses to Freud, mirrors the argument held by critics over Wilson’s interpretation; it offers a ‘reductive’ interpretation.

 

  Lacan’s theories furthered Freud’s cause by offering a re-reading of Freud; he accepts Freud’s invitation to interpret his body of work and by doing so, shifts emphasis from the sole authority of Freud.  In the light of Lacan’s work, it has perhaps now become difficult to think about the authority – and scientific credibility (or truth) - behind Freudian analysis.  Transference is acknowledged to be ‘the terrain on which all the basic problems of a given analysis play themselves out’ (Laplanche and Pontalis, 1973:455), and Lacan has brought to the forefront the structural issues of  [Freudian] psychoanalysis.  Lacan’s studies (and subsequent development) of the process of transference ensure that ‘authority’ (in the psychoanalytic sense) is not solely confined to one figure in the relationship between analyst and analysand.  So upon reading psychoanalysis through Lacan’s work, Felman’s essay becomes essentially a case study in literary transference.

 

  Felman attributes the aggressive dialectic of the opposing critics to James’s moments of ambiguity.  Her poetic criticism of the critics’ views functions by means of a dialetic of violence that she constructs from their own language, which she then traces historically to what James refers to as his ‘trap’, as the root cause.  Felman cites initial reactions to the novel, emphasising the vehement, aggressive tone of the critical dialectic deployed as ‘hopelessly evil’ or ‘distinctly repulsive’ by ‘helping to debauch … the pure and trusting nature of children’ (1982:96).  Wilson’s so-called Freudian reading of the text later arouses similar critical derision; he is criticised in how he ‘does violence’ to the novel (p.98) and must therefore be ‘attacked point-by-point’ (p.99).  So when Douglas reminds his initial audience that the story won’t tell everything in ‘any literal vulgar way’, the authority of Wilson’s Freudian analysis is finally crushed, or to quote another critic: ‘completely defeated’ (Ibid.).  The vulgarity, perhaps exists in the violent language bandied by undoubtedly intelligent and literate critics: ‘and this Jamesian commentary seems to be suggesting that such a reading might indeed be inaccurate not so much because it is incorrect or false, but because it is, in James’s terms, vulgar’.

 

  The creation of psychoanalysis creates a model for the suggestion of how other structures might have been examined.  These are ultimately metonymic discourses, where external structures of thought are built, which attempt to describe internal structures.  Psychoanalysis can (and perhaps should) be seen as a meta-language: it is a system based on a pre-existing system (language).  Psychoanalysis is a set of tools, built upon existing literature and myth, the metonymic, the unconscious of literature.  In the light of literary transference, Felman’s proposal is clear: her reading is an attempt ‘not so much to capture the mystery’s solution, but to follow, rather, the significant path of its flight’.  To understand the rhetorical functioning of textual ambiguity: ‘not “what does the story mean?” but rather “how does the story mean”’, to examine the effect of ambiguous literature and its effect of ‘permanent displacement’ on the unconscious.

Commentary on Feedback

 

The feedback advice I was given for this piece was some of the most useful I had ever received for a single piece of work.  Good feedback, I felt, deserved some kind of response, so here are a few comments on one particular comment.  I could offer comments on several of the comments made, but soon the length this commentary would outstrip the essay itself…

 

‘dealing in philosophy of interpretation’:  As I read (and later re-read) many of last term’s set readings, further readings and readings I found for the course, I noticed that much of what was done with the texts involved a degree of ‘word-play’ on behalf of the writers concerned.  It wasn’t so much what was said, but the ways in which things could be said in order to make a point more concretely.  In the light of the texts investigating Lacanian psychoanalysis, his Sassurian semiotic approach to re-reading Freud (also, Felman’s re-reading of Lacan, and therefore Wright’s re-reading of Felman’s re-reading of Lacan and so on.), involved (in certain respects) perhaps not saying a lot more than Freud (cf: Stam et al. on Lacan’s re-reading of Freud’s reading of the child’s game of fort/da), but using language to re-situate Freud’s own interpretation of the scene.  It wasn’t necessarily that the points he addressed were that much more astute than those made by Freud (in my opinion, of course), but his use of language to sew up the point Freud was making was - I felt - far more astute.  Perhaps the major difference between Freud and Lacan (and dare I make such a bold statement this early on?) then, is that Lacan’s work is packaged in such a way that it invites readings on a multitude of levels.  One sentence can have several dimensions of meaning, depending on where it is approached from...   


More simplistically: what are the differences between discover and dis-cover; represent and re-present.  On the surface, very little, as both words mean the same.  The difference lies in the way the writer is suggesting the word should be read; from where I look at it, the hyphen reminds me that the word ‘discover’ can be taken as two words and the hyphen makes that explicit (something that the eye may perhaps miss if the word is not hyphenated).  Discover is a negation of a familiar word: cover.  Likewise ‘represent’, suggests an object, which is used to symbolise another object (in film (and in some respects, literature, also) this is often referred to as symbolism and iconography is denoted by a collection of symbols and this is how generic stereotypes are surely built up: eg: the saloon doors in the Western).  Add the hyphen and we remember that it is a presentation that has taken place before and is taking place again re-present.  I could literally go on all day just by looking further into these two words. 

 

Although this was not something I mentioned in my log, the first term’s readings have been memorable in the ways in which they have - in all honesty - completely reshaped the way I now examine most situations (be they arguments, films, literature, everyday character interactions or whatever), in the light of looking at layers of meaning in what is said and how that ‘meaning’ may be interpreted depending on the stance adopted by the interpreter.


[1] The word unconscious can be taken [albeit simplistically] as a reference to mental activity that is unavailable to consciousness because it concerns material deemed too threatening for the ego to recognise directly.

[2] Lacan’s theories emerged through his experience as a training analyst.  His research is largely a result of his own dissatisfaction with the ambiguities found in Freud’s work, so as a training analyst he developed his own textual comparisons and remarks to deal with the practical experience of transference within his own work.  Felman therefore surely acknowledges a debt to Lacan’s work.
