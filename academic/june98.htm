June 1998

 

Discuss the potential impact the Internet (and modern ICTs) could have on our existing perceptions of culture and community

 

  The vast scale of the Internet moves the whole issue of society and culture into an entirely new dimension.  In 1995, the Internet had anywhere between 10 and 25 million people connected to it.  The Independent Association of Internet users known as the ‘Internet Society’ estimates that by 2001 there will be over 5 billion computers (N.B. computers, not users.  The figure for users is potentially, perhaps inevitably, even larger), connected to the network. (‘New Scientist’, 18/2/95, p.33).  However, it’s not just the network and the hardware necessary for it to operate that will see a global ‘explosion’, but the increasing capabilities of this technology, which, according to Moore’s law (1971), has been doubling in magnitude every two years.  To this day, this has proven to be correct (Gates, 1995 p.88).

  To illustrate Moore’s Law in terms of technological development, I wish to use an article featured in the Independent on Sunday: “By the end of this decade it will be possible to put 10 Bibles in a single computer chip. By 2005, a chip will be able to hold all 32 volumes of the ‘Encyclopaedia Britannica’, pictures as well as text. In 25 years time, computers will be a million times faster than they are today in a way that mimics the human brain." [1] .  Also worth considering here is the falling price of the technology available which will move IT out of offices and cyber-cafes, into every home; ‘falling PC prices may eventually rival those of televisions’ (‘Guardian Online’, 2/3/95, p.7).  The effects of falling prices are already noticeable, in that on a global scale, more PC’s than cars are being purchased. (ibid.).  

  In this essay, my main point of focus will concern modern Information Technologies and their effect on our perceptions of notions of ‘community’.  As these technologies become increasingly more transparent in the future, it is believed that they will eventually become a part of everyday life, just as Television or Radio.  At this stage, I’d like to make a point clear: the Internet is not a static entity; it is in a constant state of flux as increasing numbers of ‘digital artisans’ subscribe to it and shape its user interface.  As Bill Gates observes: ‘The Internet of today is not is not the Internet of even a short time ago … It is very hard to stay up-to-date with something so dynamic’ (Gates, 1995 p.98).  Unlike traditional media, the Net is not just a spectacle for passive consumption but also a participatory activity.  As a culture it is, to quote Richard Barbrook, a ‘DIY’ culture and one where all users are fully immersed and able to interact with other users in a given area of cyberspace, in real time.  This has led to the construction of ‘netiquette’, a protocol set down by the hosts of a given virtual arena where failure to abide to the rules results in the offending user being ousted from the room and subsequently locked out of that community. ‘As the information highway becomes society’s town square, we will come to expect it to conform to our culture’s mores’ (ibid. p.161).  

  Cultures are shaped and moulded by the technologies in use at any particular time. The launch of television for example, has changed our leisure pursuits, the way we spend our free time and even family dynamics – who is in control of the channel watched and any given time.  Similarly, the Internet, computers, and IT can be seen to influence the way we behave, how we choose to spend our time and money and our social behavioural patterns and relationships.  However, it is impossible to predict any set trend as use will vary from person to person according to a large set of variables, such as exposure to technology at work or at school, interest, access to hardware and software, finances available, language and literacy issues etc.

   Since 1995, the Internet has become the iconic technology of our age.  Through its adoption of pseudo sci-fi and hyperbolic techno-slang, ‘Wired’ magazine has achieved an almost global status through its persistent claims that the ‘Net’ will create new markets and communities.  Everyone will be able to buy and sell in cyberspace without restrictions.  States and governments will no longer be able to control electronic information or commerce, which can now freely cross national borders without the need of a passport.  It seems to me that the Internet is America’s attempt to allow the world to cash in on the American dream of materialism and free speech.  

  The Internet is very much an American creation.  It evolved from a computer system built 25 years ago by the Military to enable academic and military researchers to continue to do government work even if part of the network were taken out in a nuclear attack.  Like many Cold War inventions, the Net could have remained an official secret.  However, according to Barbrook, when it was being developed within the universities, academics and students hijacked the new technology for their own purposes [2].

  In terms of the Internet and its applications, from on-line discussion groups and electronic mail to the Web, it is certainly the case that many of its features were initially developed by amateur enthusiasts as shareware, with no real obligation to pay to use them. This non-commercial ethos attracted others who began to develop the Internet as a new form of community media.  It was the growth in this initial ‘DIY culture’ that caused large software manufacturers to invest heavily in developing and marketing commercial browser software.  The legal war between Netware and Microsoft over control of the Internet that resulted in Microsoft facing a ‘$1m a day fine imposed by US federal government for abuse of monopoly power’ (Source: BBC Website 14/11/97), clearly stressing the importance of the Internet in America.  In the so-called ‘browser war’, Microsoft has been accused of employing ‘unfair practices’ with the bundled distribution of its Internet Explorer with the Windows 95 operating system, not allowing for users to purchase their Internet software from the range of companies who were previously offering web browsing facilities.  This is alarming, especially as 85% of computers purchased for home nowadays are IBM compatible PC’s (‘Guardian Online’, 2/3/95, p.7).  Interestingly, the company has avoided being accused of such tactics in the future by ensuring that Internet capabilities are embedded into Windows 98, rendering Web browsing an integral part of their future software releases.  Indeed, Microsoft has been accused of ‘trying to hijack the Internet to convert it from an open information highway to a private toll road’ (ibid. 18/11/97).  The reason that Microsoft and other corporations are now pouring billions of dollars into cyberspace is because they have to catch up with the widespread use of the Net by the state-funded institutions and the DIY culture. 

  I do not believe that the Internet will offer the world an unregulated marketplace.  Its brief history already shows the convergence of state, commercial and community interests within the emerging digital structure.  Each sector will depend on the other, in order to exist and each will have a role to fulfil.  Certainly in the UK, I find it unlikely that a ‘national data grid’ will be established, linking all households and businesses unless the public applies heavy pressure.  If it is left to unregulated market forces to offer universal access to new information, it will take a long time, if it ever even happens.  However, the construction of the fibre-optic grid, covering most of the UK implies the definite commercial potential of the Internet.  Unlike its predecessors, the BBC, British Telecom etc, which flourished under state ownership and control, the Internet is not, nor ever likely to become, even partially state controlled.  

  The problem at the moment is not the technology itself, but the distribution of new technology.  The Internet connects more than 100 countries, but there are around 90 more outside it. The have-nots all number among the world’s poorer countries (‘Guardian Online’, 7/7/94, p.7).  Despite Clinton’s promise to ‘grant’ access of the Internet to the developing world, it is apparent that there is still a long way to go. Despite it’s claims to be the new global information highway, the Internet’s map of the world resembles a medieval mariners’ chart.  Most of Africa is blank, the Far East has conspicuous holes, and Eastern Europe is scarcely chartered, while South America is just sketched in. (ibid.).  However, where there is access it can be seen as being positive. For example, in Korea and Taiwan, more than 25% of PC sales are destined for home rather than commercial use. (‘Guardian Online’, 2/3/95, p.7)  

  Digital communication holds great implications for the way the country is run, both on a local as well as a national level. Emailing will help in improving communication links between representative and the electorate, as already demonstrated by Cambridge’s Anne Campbell: ‘Direct Digital democracy has a role to play in the running of neighbourhoods. For example it could be used to ensure that tenants become involved in how their estate is managed and what new services they require. It would relocate intelligence on how the public service is run from producer to the consumer. This process of democratisation would be a massive cultural shift for deferential, hierarchical UK’ (‘Wired Magazine’, Sept. 95, p.46).  

  Ease of access to politicians entails greater involvement on the part of citizens; it is now possible to put your views across directly to the source. This is a direct example of the nature of new technologies; they are ‘not in themselves good or bad; it is the way they are used that determines value.’ [3] The Net is often seen as a ‘utopian space’, a ‘nowhere-somewhere’, which could ultimately serve as an arena where we will be able to recover the meaning and the experience of community.  Rheingold believes that we have ‘access to a tool that could bring conviviality and understanding into our lives and might help revitalise the public sphere’ (Rheingold, 1994).  We shall be able to rebuild the neighbourhood community and the small-town public sphere.  In a world in which every citizen is networked to every other citizen, we can expand this ideal to the scale of McLuhan’s ‘global village’; a vision of electronic community as the ‘good society’ (Robins, 1995) . 

  However, I believe that even when the whole population is eventually on-line, this ‘utopia’ of ‘direct’ democracy will still face the most important obstacle of all: the problem of how large numbers of people can communicate and collectively make decisions. I cannot believe that a new technology can solve fundamental social and political problems by itself.  Even if the Internet eliminates the physical limitations of space and time by gathering citizens together in one place by creating virtual spaces, it cannot remove the entirely social problem of how large groups of people can successfully interact with one another.  From my experience of using mIRC, an Internet ‘Relay Chat’ program, I often found it frustrating that I could not hold a meaningful conversation with anyone when everyone was fighting for ‘air-time’.  Unless a virtual ‘room’ only consists of a small number of people, some form of representation will have to be used to mediate between the different social, cultural and geographical groups wishing to shape political decision-making, particularly on a large scale.  Aside from this, wiring up the country will never eliminate the need for professional politicians, even if it does change the way in which we can interface with them.  

  When considering IT as a global phenomenon, difficulties arise when we begin to consider its natural bias, or at least heavy dependence, on the English language.  Although on some of the more global web pages it is possible to select your own language from a largely restricted list of alternatives, full access is granted to those who are largely based in a Western society and can understand the Internet’s ‘global’ language.  People who are not native English speakers are forced to adapt to this American technology, and thus adopt the English language in order to be able to navigate freely through cyberspace.  For a large demographic of the World, even the alphabet on a PC keyboard comprises of foreign symbols.  Therefore, even though such far-off locations, for example, remote parts of China will soon be connected, those who are not competent in English will, ultimately, remain excluded and in turn silenced within the global, even national vision of ‘community’.  In an increasingly globalised society, this problem will have to be tackled first, in order for the Internet to become a truly global information superhighway.  

   However, accessibility for the blind has already been aided by the development of a ‘WebSpeak browser’. [4]   The browser works by reading out the text on the screen, describing hypertext links; it can also be used to read e-mail, handle file transfers and can be plugged into any ‘text-to-speech’ products already available, and can be installed on a windows based PC like any other browser. It will be distributed freely, in the spirit of free speech advocated by the Internet, as shareware, and holds great potential in ‘schools and libraries, to allow blind and partially sighted users to learn alonside others’ according to Ray Ingram, executive vice president of ‘Productivity Works’.  

  When we consider the fact that, to some extent, solutions have already been developed for those with constraints other than language, bridging the language barrier would seem to be the next logical step forward. With the developing world and non-English speaking community the IT giants such as Microsoft have an immense and lucrative source of custom, they cannot ignore it in order to remain operative in a global society.   

  A problem that must be tackled before we even look at the question of access is literacy.  In the UK Internet Book, Sue Schofield observes that: ‘According to a recent US Adult Literacy Report, adult literacy has plummeted to just 50 per cent in the US which has a population of around two hundred and fifty million people’ (Schofield, 1995).  Computerisation of society has often been blamed for this trend, but forms of communication such as email via computers necessitate literacy. As such, computers can be seen to promote language skills, when considering computers outside factory applications for example.  

   The Internet has played a role in redefining our sense of culture and thus identity, on a social and global scale. Extensive media coverage conveys the importance ascribed to IT in all facets of life.  Positive developments such as low priced PCs and the introduction of public cyber-cafes such as Brighton’s ‘Cybar’ are helping the whole of society to gain access to communication and information tools that are universally applicable mean that the world is now operating on the most level playing field ever seen.  Increasing globalisation of the Internet and it’s applications reinforce this situation - with all connected countries having to assess their place in a dynamic and all-encompassing global arena.   

  IT is the key to the future of our society and one of the most important factors influencing this view is the universality of agreement - the media agrees, the Developed World agrees, the Developing World agrees. The possibilities are endless.  The Internet potentially offers hope to millions left behind by today’s society - the disabled, the unemployed, the elderly etc.  Access does not necessarily have to rely on knowledge or financial circumstances; more and more provisions are being made, such as the introduction of Internet terminals into public libraries and cyber-cafes, to cater for those who cannot afford to buy a PC outright.  

  In an age of increasing unemployment, falling social standards and increased poverty, the Net fills a gap. Communication between peers, which might be denied to those who are not in work for example, is possible via email, newsgroups etc. The Internet is also a useful tool to maintain contact with home, or family, in a time when there is increasing commuting within jobs, and long distance travel is a [sometimes a day to day] fact rather than a rarity.  

  The Internet permits the fragmentation of society in that common links can be made between any groups around any common factor, from sense of nationality, to liking a particular science fiction programme. It is much more difficult to define people according to set boundaries, as the parameters by which people identify themselves have shifted boundlessly. The fear that IT will bring about homogeneity and a global Americanisation does not necessarily have to hold true - although I think it is likely to happen - unless people decide freely to subscribe to that common identity. Hollywood may have dominated much of popular culture up to now, but with the increasing emphasis on personalised entertainment, this era might be over. Europe for example, has a rich cultural heritage, as does Asia, with increasing numbers of people trained and willing to distribute this information on the Internet, providing an alternative to a bland homogenised culture promoted by the Movies.  Thus, diversity is in-built in the experience of communicating and operating on the Internet: ‘the Internet mediates human interaction better than any other medium’, says futurist Paul Saffo: ‘Getting in touch with each other is more fun than the coolest computer game, or the hottest information’. [5] Stephen Fry supports this view: ‘when you have a keyboard under your fingers, you can say things that are extraordinarily honest. This is one of the wonderful things about e-mail that is often overlooked’. (‘Evening Standard’, 3/11/95). In what can be seen as a reinforcement of this phenomenon, Mark Poster argues that message services have proven themselves immensely popular in France.  The country’s 1980s on-line system, ‘Minitel’, which was similar in content and style to the British ‘Prestel’ system was believed to foster ‘not the autonomous, rational, stable individual but the playful, imaginative multiple self’.  (Poster, 1995).  He argued that ‘people seem to enjoy a communication technology in which they invent themselves in the process of exchanging signs’. (ibid.).  Although honesty and the imagined self might seem to be conflicting, the connection between the two is the movement towards new modes of communication which do not play a part in normal face-to-face interaction. This can only be seen as a bonus in that reinvention of society necessitates new forms of communication, fostered by the Internet.  

  For many people, participation in an on-line chat network can be a positive life changing experience. They can choose whether or not to disclose their disability (if they have one) and they also have the advantage of anonymity in this unique social situation. For one who may have a low-self image due to physical appearance, or in cases of extreme shyness in social situations, this becomes almost a non-issue on-line. Other users cannot see what a person looks like, nor can they identify discomfort in posture or the hesitant speech of someone with extreme shyness. This is a unique new social situation and the lack of a synchronous Face To Face presence gives the actor far greater control over the developing definition of the situation.  The user has an opportunity to present the ‘ideal self’ and sustain that presentation throughout the interaction.  The barriers in communication discussed above are not present when sitting behind a monitor and keyboard.  In such situations, people have the opportunity to practice communicating with others, portraying themselves with more confidence than they may actually have in reality. This can be a good thing, as this can be a system of positive re-enforcement of social behaviour, learned in an on-line environment, which they can then carry out into their real lives.  However, this has disadvantages for somebody like myself, where I will often dismiss most of what I am told over the Internet, as I am aware of the fabrication of truth.  

  On a lesser scale, IT is the key to the future on an individual basis, as well as on a national and international level.  Giving people the keys to cyberspace improves their self-esteem and makes them feel more involved in society, according to the preliminary results of an experiment in Florida in which six low-income families were lent computers and given unlimited access to the Internet. [6] (‘New Scientist’, 17/2/96, p.11).  The proficiency gained from continuous practice led to a reported feeling of belonging, playing a part in society: ‘It really did in some way allow them to transcend poverty and disability’ (ibid.).  One woman, worried that her computer would be removed after the power cable had been chewed through by a rat, pleaded to be allowed to keep the computer, saying ‘I know that I’m in poverty. But when I get on the Internet, nobody else knows it’ (ibid.).  In the same way that anonymity makes dating so much less fraught on the Net, so this same quality transcends traditional social boundaries. Where acquisition of technology used to be seen as a way of “keeping up with the Joneses”, now the increasing ease of access could potentially render social divides invisible, at least while the user is on-line.  Also, with the inclusion of Internet technology with the Digital Terrestrial TV network, due to come on-line later this year, it should become possible for anyone to experience a society without the traditional enforced hierarchical boundaries.  Anonymity is the key to a more tolerant society; hierarchical bias and prejudice cannot play a part in communication when there is no knowledge of the circumstances between any two people communicating at one time.  

  In February this year, Microsoft piloted a scheme called ‘Cyberstreet’.  This ultimately served as a test of whether an entire North London street could be linked up on the Internet, creating a localised cyber-community.  The Islington road was selected as an ideal location for such a scheme, due to the wide mix of class, age and ethnicity.  Thirty people, supposedly a wide demographic cross-section, were connected to the ‘street’ and soon a community began to build.  Microsoft’s representatives met up with the users once a week to discuss ways of improving the community, and to monitor its development in general.  In terms of enforcing notions of community, there was sufficient evidence to show that this was the case: ‘Neighbours on the Net said they [the community] had already held one drinks party and planned a street party’ (‘The Big Issue’, Feb. 98).  The ‘street’ was criticised by The Big Issue, who claimed it was ‘for middle class only’.  The report stated that: ‘the experiment was supposed to span the whole community, but it seems council tenants and pensioners have been ignored in favour of middle-class home owners with 2.4 children’.  

  If culture and community is thus civilisation, then to quote Jeremy Paxman: ‘civilisation is simply mass hallucination’ (BBC2 ‘London Debate’ 28/4/98).  Therefore civilisation and culture and community are perceived within the minds of the individual.  The mind of the individual is affected by information, regardless of the carrier media.  When the individual is multiplied to make the masses, it becomes obvious that our perceived notions of culture and community will be affected by relentless access to information, or at least, growth in access to information, the Internet embodies.  Therefore, our notions of what ‘culture’ and ‘community’ mean, are, to some extent, safeguarded by politics and governments.  This has previously been done by limiting access to information, either by selective education in terms of a ‘weighted’ curriculum and (or) through censorship.  The carrier medium of the future is theoretically free of such ‘weighted’ emphasis, which will inevitably contra the intentions of the so-called guardians by allowing more access to ‘free’ information.  

  The future of our society depends on the constant development of IT.  As the next decade comes into play, we are likely to see more and more electrical devices with on-line capabilities.  For example, the release of Digital Terrestrial Television in the UK later this year will ultimately fuse together the Internet with television.  For the creation of set-top DTT boxes, it is understood that the BBC are working closely with Microsoft to develop the hardware for the new system.  Also, Microsoft’s development of the ‘Windows CE’ interface means that pocket organisers will now have ‘on-line’ capabilities such as Internet access and e-mail.  Another area that will undoubtedly be affected by technological change is the office.  I believe that the next millennium will see the implementation of the ‘virtual office’, where people will work from home on their computers and upload their work to the office computer when it is completed.  This will undoubtedly have many benefits, firstly, with women believed to outnumber men in the next millennium, Mothers will be able to work from home whilst also supervising their children.  The virtual office will also cut commuting time to a minimum, where both work and board meetings can be conducted from home.  Finally, I can already a see a change in employment ethics.  Jobs in the future are far more likely to be based on short-term contracts where, rather than stay working in one job until retirement, skills will be carried from one job to the next and people will carry with them ‘portfolios’ of their previous employment.  This seems to me to be more like a self-employed profession, such as that for an actor or freelance writer.  I can see a definite tendency towards self-employment where more people manage their own careers, working on short-term contracts.
